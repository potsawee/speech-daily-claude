### 1. ★ Decoupling Conversational Dynamics in Full-Duplex Spoken Models through Reinforcement Learning — `2607.07148` · **Relevance 9/10 · Quality 8/10**
*Yuxin Li, Donghang Wu, Guan-Ting Lin, **Hung-yi Lee**, Chengwei Qin, Zhehuai Chen, Chen Chen (Nanyang Technological University; National Taiwan University; HKUST; NVIDIA) · eess.AS · 2026-07-08 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.07148)*

*Problem:* Full-duplex spoken dialogue models gain natural timing — backchannels, low latency, barge-in — but tend to lose reasoning and instruction-following, an apparent intelligence-vs-dynamics trade-off.
*Method:* DuplexPO, an RL framework that decouples *when to speak* from *what to say*; a Factorized Conversational Dynamics Reward gives fine-grained temporal credit for turn initiation, backchanneling, and yielding, optimized with a GRPO-style objective.
*Result:* Substantially improves full-duplex behaviors while preserving reasoning and instruction-following, with the gains reflected in better user experience.
**Why it ranked:** Followed author Hung-yi Lee (NTU) on the hottest spoken-LM subtopic — full-duplex dialogue — with a genuinely novel decoupling that dissolves the intelligence–dynamics tension and a clean reward design (NVIDIA co-authors).

### 2. ★ Compress the Cache, Not the Speech Embedding: KV Compression for Efficient Speech LLMs — `2607.06827` · **Relevance 9/10 · Quality 8/10**
*Ke-Han Lu, Keqi Deng, Ruchao Fan, Rui Zhao, **Jinyu Li** (Microsoft, with National Taiwan University & University of Cambridge) · eess.AS/cs.SD · 2026-07-07 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.06827)*

*Problem:* Speech LLMs encode audio into sequences far longer than text, bottlenecking autoregressive decoding; compressing at the adapter level risks discarding fine-grained, unrecoverable information.
*Method:* SpeechKV applies a learned pooling to the KV cache of speech tokens *inside* the LLM, letting it fuse speech and text internally while compressing to roughly text-level granularity. Trained on 71K hours.
*Result:* Matches or slightly beats the uncompressed baseline (+6.6% out-of-domain entity recognition, +2.3% OpenASR) with ≥1.49× decoding speedup that scales with audio length.
**Why it ranked:** Followed author Jinyu Li (Microsoft) delivering a practical, well-validated speech-LLM efficiency win squarely in the user's speech-LM wheelhouse; submitted to SLT2026.

### 3. Gradient-Based Speech-to-Text Alignment for Any ASR Model: From CTC to Speech LLMs — `2607.06831` · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 8/10**
*Albert Zeyer, Ralf Schlüter, Hermann Ney (RWTH Aachen University) · cs.CL/cs.LG · 2026-07-07 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.06831)*

*Problem:* Word-level speech-to-text timing is built into CTC/transducer models but not attention encoder-decoders or speech LLMs, whose timings are read off the coarse encoder-grid attention.
*Method:* A training-free, model-agnostic aligner: take the gradient of each teacher-forced token's log-probability w.r.t. the input, reduce to per-frame saliency, and decode word boundaries with a single dynamic-programming pass on the fine input grid.
*Result:* Produces usable alignments for all 16 models across four families including speech LLMs, beating native aligners where those are weak (e.g. streaming); cost is one backward pass per token.
**Why it ranked:** Core ASR/speech-LLM relevance plus a rare general, training-free method rigorously validated across 16 models and four families, from Ney and Schlüter's authoritative RWTH Aachen ASR group.

### 4. Ranking the Impact of Contextual Specialization in Neural Speech Enhancement — `2607.04826` · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 8/10**
*Peter Leer, Svend Feldt, Zheng-Hua Tan, Jan Østergaard, Jesper Jensen (Aalborg University, Denmark) · eess.AS/cs.SD · 2026-07-06 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.04826)*

*Problem:* When a neural speech enhancer specializes to context — speaker identity, noise type, gender, language, or SNR — which factor actually delivers the gains?
*Method:* Fine-tune generalist enhancers (~10k to ~5M parameters) on data subsets defined by each contextual attribute and rank the resulting speech-intelligibility and quality improvements, including cross-lingual tests.
*Result:* Speaker-identity specialization gives the largest gains; a small model specialized to a speaker plus noise type matches a generalist 10× its size, and language-specialized models beat multilingual generalists.
**Why it ranked:** Central speech-enhancement topic with a rigorous, memorable finding — speaker-specialized small models beat 10× generalists — and clear on-device (hearing-aid) relevance from a credible Aalborg team.

### 5. Text-Independent Speaker Verification Using Discrete Audio Tokens — `2607.07579` · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 7/10**
*Zheng Liang, Junjie Li, Kong Aik Lee (The Hong Kong Polytechnic University) · eess.AS · 2026-07-08 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.07579)*

*Problem:* Discrete neural-codec tokens power speech synthesis, but their representations consistently underperform traditional spectral features for automatic speaker verification (ASV).
*Method:* Show empirically that speaker cues survive in codec tokens but are underused, then apply Cross-Feature Knowledge Distillation (CFKD) to train the codec-based student to mimic a strong Fbank teacher's embedding space.
*Result:* On the VoxCeleb benchmarks, CFKD substantially improves codec-based ASV, letting it approach the Fbank teacher's accuracy — evidence discrete audio tokens can serve speaker tasks, not just synthesis.
**Why it ranked:** Bridges two of the user's core interests — audio codecs/tokenization and speaker verification — with a clean distillation fix and solid VoxCeleb gains from Kong Aik Lee's group.
