### 1. ★ Improving Text-to-Audio Instruction Following via Fine-Grained Feedback from Audio-Aware Large Language Models — `2607.13408` · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 8/10**
*Chun-Yi Kuan, Siwon Kim, Byeonggeun Kim, Suyoun Kim, Bo-Ru Lu, Qinming Tang, Ankur Gandhe, **Hung-yi Lee** ★, Chieh-Chi Kao, Chao Wang — Amazon AGI (with National Taiwan University) · eess.AS/cs.CL/cs.SD · 2026-07-15 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.13408)*

*Problem:* Text-to-audio models produce high-quality sound but often botch instructions involving multiple sound events and their temporal order, because training and evaluation signals reward global similarity or perceptual quality rather than instruction-level correctness.
*Method:* Audio-aware LLMs act as fine-grained judges that verify target-event presence and temporal relations in generated audio; after validating those verdicts against benchmarks and human ratings, the judgments build preference pairs for direct preference optimization, and a new narrative benchmark, S3Bench, probes multi-event temporal following.
*Result:* The recipe improves event completeness, temporal ordering, and joint instruction-following accuracy across existing benchmarks and S3Bench while preserving audio quality.
**Why it ranked:** ★ followed (Hung-yi Lee) and an Amazon collaboration squarely on the user's text-to-audio / audio-LLM interest; a clean ALLM-as-judge→DPO recipe with human-validated feedback and a purpose-built temporal-following benchmark.

### 2. ★ The Sound of Absence: Audio-Language Embedding Models Struggle with Negation — `2607.12290` · **Relevance 7/10 · Quality 7/10**
*Chun-Yi Kuan, **Hung-yi Lee** ★ — National Taiwan University · eess.AS/cs.AI/cs.SD · 2026-07-14 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.12290)*

*Problem:* CLAP-style audio-language embedding models are almost always evaluated on matching present sounds, hiding whether they can represent absent or negated concepts at all.
*Method:* NegEval-Audio converts existing datasets into two negation-aware tasks — Retrieval-Neg and Multiple-Choice Negation — to test whether models separate present from absent events, and probes a training-free steering fix.
*Result:* On AudioCaps and Clotho, performance collapses under negation (negation-type MCQ accuracy falls far below chance) even for a recent multimodal-LLM embedding model; steering helps MCQ-Neg but barely helps retrieval, pinning affirmation bias as a fundamental flaw in the representation geometry that needs explicit negation-aware objectives.
**Why it ranked:** ★ followed (Hung-yi Lee) and on-profile (CLAP / audio-language embeddings); a clean, well-designed probe that surfaces an actionable, previously-hidden blind spot in current audio embeddings.

### 3. ★ Do LLMs Need Architectural Changes for Simultaneous Speech Translation? A Prefix-to-Prefix Data-Driven Approach — `2607.13158` · **Relevance 7/10 · Quality 6/10**
*Junkun Chen, Jian Xue, Ming Tang, Abdel Heba, Hoda Gholami, Ruchao Fan, **Jinyu Li** ★ — Microsoft · cs.CL · 2026-07-14 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.13158)*

*Problem:* Simultaneous speech translation with decoder-only LLMs usually needs bespoke architectures or explicit read/write policies, which turn brittle when conversational segmentation boundaries are ambiguous.
*Method:* A purely data-driven alternative — fixed-length chunk streaming with a rewind-based committed prefix, plus teacher-labeled prefix-to-prefix (P2P) targets with bounded waiting for fine-tuning — trains a chunked streaming speech-encoder LLM (CSSEL-P2P) with no architectural change.
*Result:* On in-house conversational speech, CSSEL-P2P improves streaming quality by +1.54 COMETKiwi over the CSSEL streaming baseline at comparable latency (+0.15s average lagging), showing effective SimulST from P2P supervision alone.
**Why it ranked:** ★ followed (Jinyu Li) and on-profile (streaming speech translation); a tidy, architecture-free result from Microsoft speech, though evaluation is in-house only and the margin is modest.

### 4. Audio-Native Speech Recognition with a Frozen Discrete-Diffusion Language Model — `2607.13013` · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 6/10**
*Harsha Vardhan Khurdula, Abhinav Kumar Singh, Yoeven D Khemlani, Vineet Agarwal — affiliation not disclosed · cs.AI/cs.SD · 2026-07-14 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.13013)*

*Problem:* ASR is dominated by autoregressive decoders that emit one token at a time; can a discrete-diffusion language model instead refine an entire transcript in parallel over a handful of denoising steps?
*Method:* A frozen Whisper encoder feeds acoustic features through a lightweight projector into a frozen 26B discrete-diffusion LLM via LoRA (~42M trainable params, 0.16% of the backbone); the key trick is a CTC loss applied through the frozen output head, which grounds the audio when the natural diffusion objectives fail to.
*Result:* 6.6% word error rate on LibriSpeech test-clean, transcribing in roughly eight parallel steps regardless of utterance length, with a single adapter covering English, Hindi, and Mandarin.
**Why it ranked:** A genuinely novel, ambitious take on non-autoregressive ASR (parallel diffusion decoding plus a clever CTC-through-frozen-head fix) directly on the user's ASR / speech-LM interest — featured for the idea despite an undisclosed affiliation, no released code, and a WER that is not yet SOTA.

### 5. Auditing Protocol-Level Shortcuts in Large Audio Language Model Judges for Speech Evaluation — `2607.13477` · **Relevance 7/10 · Quality 7/10**
*Joonyong Park, David M. Chan, Yuki Saito, Hiroshi Saruwatari — The University of Tokyo (with UC Berkeley) · cs.SD/cs.CL/eess.AS · 2026-07-15 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.13477)*

*Problem:* Large audio-language models are increasingly used as automatic judges for speech, but high agreement with human ratings does not prove their verdicts are grounded in the audio rather than in labels or references the evaluation protocol itself supplies.
*Method:* Across three deployment protocols — feature-blueprint judging, reference-conditioned judging, and pairwise A/B comparison — the authors audit six LALM judges on four attributes using matched shortcut probes, e.g. corrupting specialist labels or swapping A/B order.
*Result:* Several judges take shortcuts: wrong specialist labels crash five judges' emotion accuracy to ≤0.10, and Qwen3-Omni-Thinking often picks the same A/B slot regardless of order — so aggregate agreement can badly overstate validity unless model and protocol are audited jointly.
**Why it ranked:** On-profile audio-LLM evaluation with a rigorous, timely cautionary finding for anyone using LALMs as speech judges; a careful multi-protocol, multi-judge design from a credible lab.
