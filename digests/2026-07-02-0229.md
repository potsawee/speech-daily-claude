### ★ 1. Reference-Based Prosody and Rhythm Evaluation for Spoken Dialogue Systems — 2606.31055 · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 7.5/10**
**Authors:** Ashish Hallur, Thomas Thebaud, Georgi Tinchev, Venkatesh Ravichandran, Laureano Moro-Velazquez — Johns Hopkins University (CLSP) / Amazon · cs.CL, cs.SD, eess.AS · 2026-07-01 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.31055)
*Problem:* Speech-to-speech agents lack interpretable, speech-native metrics for conversational prosody/rhythm; pooled human statistics mis-calibrate because F0, rate, and pausing shift with speaker traits and interaction state.
*Method:* Using 4000+ hrs of dyadic English (Seamless Interaction), build matched reference regimes for six prosodic metrics, then score an output's percentile deviation vs. its closest matched human stratum with 5th–95th out-of-regime flags.
*Result:* Matched references bring flag rates near the nominal 10% and give interpretable deviation direction, whereas pooled references over-flag state-conditioned expressivity and rhythm.
**Why it ranked:** ★ followed lab (Johns Hopkins CLSP) and squarely on-interest (prosody + evaluation); a principled, dataset-grounded protocol that fills a real gap in S2S-agent evaluation.

### 2. Elastic Time: Dynamic Frame Rate Bottlenecks for Neural Audio Coding — 2606.27320 · **Relevance 8/10 · Quality 8/10**
**Authors:** Dimitrios Bralios, Paris Smaragdis, Minje Kim — University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign & Adobe Research · cs.SD, cs.LG, eess.AS · 2026-06-26 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.27320)
*Problem:* Most neural audio autoencoders run at a fixed latent frame rate, spending equal temporal budget on regions of very different information density and producing needlessly long token sequences.
*Method:* Elastic Time adds a dynamic frame-rate bottleneck that converts fixed-rate autoencoders to dynamic ones via a lightweight latent predictor deciding which frames to skip and later reconstruct, with greedy boundary selection at inference.
*Result:* Enables deployment-time rate control and improves the efficiency–quality tradeoff over fixed-rate baselines, shortening sequences for downstream generation and long-context modeling.
**Why it ranked:** Directly on the audio-codec/tokenizer interest with a genuinely novel adaptive-rate mechanism, from a strong track-record group (Smaragdis/Kim) — the kind of idea that matters for audio LMs.

### 3. Attacking UTMOS: Probing the Robustness of a Speech Quality Assessment Model — 2606.31105 · **Relevance 7/10 · Quality 7.5/10**
**Authors:** Wen-Chin Huang, Tomoki Toda — Nagoya University · cs.SD, eess.AS · 2026-07-01 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.31105)
*Problem:* UTMOS is now a default DNN-based speech-quality (MOS) metric across the field, yet its robustness is largely untested — a risk given how widely it gates TTS/VC/enhancement results.
*Method:* Adversarially optimize inputs in two directions (score-preserving = degrade quality but hold score; quality-preserving = drop score but hold quality) across three input spaces: raw waveform, mel+HiFi-GAN, and EnCodec latent space.
*Result:* Score-preserving attacks are highly effective; quality-preserving attacks are harder but most succeed in EnCodec's latent space — exposing concrete failure modes of the metric.
**Why it ranked:** A clean, cautionary robustness probe of a metric almost everyone in speech relies on; relevant and rigorous, with a nice EnCodec-latent twist, from Toda's lab.

### 4. Enhancing BEST-RQ Pseudo-Label Quality through Online Refinement for ASR — 2606.30671 · **Relevance 7.5/10 · Quality 7.5/10**
**Authors:** Jingjing Xu, Zijian Yang, Mohammad Zeineldeen, Eugen Beck, Ralf Schlüter, Hermann Ney — RWTH Aachen University / AppTek · cs.SD, eess.AS · 2026-07-01 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.30671)
*Problem:* BEST-RQ's fixed online quantizer keeps SSL pretraining simple but gives weaker supervision than HuBERT-style iterative pseudo-label refinement.
*Method:* Three simplicity-preserving tweaks to online pseudo-label generation — replace the quantizer's linear projection with PCA, iteratively refine the codebook, and add a second codebook updated via codebook distillation.
*Result:* On LibriSpeech (960h pretrain, 100h fine-tune) all three combined give a 12% relative WER cut on test-other (10.1%→8.8%).
**Why it ranked:** On-interest SSL/ASR work with a concrete, reproducible LibriSpeech gain from the well-regarded RWTH group; incremental but clearly useful for the BEST-RQ line.

### 5. PATSE: Position-Aware Target Speaker Extraction for Long-Form Multi-Party Conversations — 2606.29497 · **Relevance 7.5/10 · Quality 7.5/10**
**Authors:** Yichi Wang, Junzhe Chen, Wangjin Zhou, Tatsuya Kawahara — Kyoto University · cs.SD, cs.MM · 2026-06-30 · [Link](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.29497)
*Problem:* In long meetings, imbalanced speaker activity and overlap make "who spoke when and what" hard; sliding-window CSS suffers cross-window inconsistency and crosstalk, forcing a separate diarization stage.
*Method:* PATSE is a multi-channel front-end that uses each speaker's direction-of-arrival as a spatial prior — a DOA-guided spatial encoder/conditioner extracts speaker-attributed streams, with activity inferred by simple VAD post-processing, no explicit diarization.
*Result:* On replayed and real conversations, consistent ASR gains over both CSS and diarization-based pipelines.
**Why it ranked:** Diarization-free target-speaker extraction is squarely on-interest, and the DOA-prior design is elegant and practical; solid meeting-ASR contribution from Kawahara's lab.
